Applied Mathematics II: Economics

In this volume, we turn consider mathematical applications in the field of economics. Alongside physics, economics is a particularly insightful area because of its contributions to game theory. As we'll see, economics has contributed a great deal to pure mathematics itself, with many leading economists hailing from extensive mathematics backgrounds. Moreover, much of the materials we explore in this volume are directly applicable to daily life. Economic principles dictate a substantial portion of our decisions, as well as others'. We begin by stating the foundational axioms of economics — propositions that will guide our mathematical analyses and dicussions.

Scarcity

For every hour we spend studying for an exam, that's an hour spent not doing something else. That “something else” could be making plans for a new business, designing a new product, or learning a trade. This is because time is scarce. But time isn't the only scare resource. Investing ${\$1~000~000}$ into company ${x}$ is ${\$1 ~000~000}$ not spent on company ${y.}$ ${5\text{s}}$ spent performing algorithm ${A}$ is ${5\text{s}}$ not spent performing algorithm ${B.}$
