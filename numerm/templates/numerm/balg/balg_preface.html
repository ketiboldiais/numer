Classical Algebra

The materials that follow explore classical algebra. The word algebra is derived from the Arabic al jebr, which loosely translates to “reunion” (corresponding to the mathematicians of Baghdad gathering the terms of equations). The word al jebr gradually evolved into algebrista, the term for a barber in Medieval span. At the time, barbers were also bone-setters; they “reunited” bones. To understand what classical algebra is, we must define (even if crudely) what algebra is in general. Specific to mathematics, we can think of algebra as akin to the syntax of mathematics — the set of all rules defining when a proposition has meaning. Algebra defines what we can and cannot do with symbols, as well as how to rewrite our propositions. With a strong grounding in algebra, numerous ideas in mathematics suddenly become accessible, which in turn allows us to further explore the mathematical universe. There's nothing wrong with this thinking, but it's somewhat naive. It doesn't entirely capture what algebra really is.

A more sophisticated view: algebra is the study of algebraic structures (also called algebras). An algebraic structure, or algebra, is a closed system consisting of a set, and operations on that set. Those operations can come in the form of rules, properties, limitations, restrictions, exceptions, tests, and a myriad of other words humans come up with. They define what we can and cannot do with members of the algebra's set. Viewed in this manner, algebras exist everywhere in both reality and the abstract.

Chess is an algebra. There is a set — the chess pieces — and there are operations on that set — pawn can only move forward, bishop diagonally, rook in straight lines, etc. The Internal Revenue Code, which defines when tax liability occurs, is an algebra. There is a set (increases in financial wealth), and there are operations on that set (a deduction here, a loss there, a capital gain then, exclude this, report that). A class in an object-oriented programming language like Java is an algebra. It has instances (the set) and methods (the operations). Or perhaps even closer to home, a course grading policy is an algebra. There is a set (the student's grades) and operations on that set (when to curve, when to include extra credit, when to subtract points for lateness, when to add points for participation, etc.).

Algebras define what propositions “make sense” in the algebra. For example, in the algebra of criminal law, the proposition “The jury returned an innocent verdict” does not make sense, at least formally (and in algebra, we keep things formal). Since a verdict denies or affirms a defendant's presumption of innocence, a verdict can only be guilty, not guilty, or neither (it only takes one to hang a federal jury). In the algebra of the Swift language's Int protocol, the statement var a: Int = 1 + 2.3 makes no sense, because 2.3 is a floating point number, not an integer.

For most people, the classical algebra looks familiar. However, unless one has had experience with linear algebra, set theory, or programming, the latter propositions likely look blatantly wrong. All the propositions, however, are meaningful in their algebras. Recall that we said algebras are closed systems. The rules in one algebra may be completely different and inapplicable in another. One proposition means one thing here, and an entirely different thing there. It's not that one system is more wrong or more right than the other; they're just different closed systems. Propositions having one meaning in one system and an entirely different meaning in another system aren't all that odd. Consider this proposition: “The British left waffles on Falklands.” At its highest levels, the study of algebra is about untangling these systems, and finding connections between them.

Before reaching these peak altitudes, we must start at the foot of the mountain — classical algebra. This is the algebra most people are familiar with, and it is the oldest of the mathematical algebras. It is the algebra taught in early mathematics education, whether that's elementary, high school, or a first-year course in college. It is what non-mathematicians simply call “algebra,” and what some schools (unhelpfully) call “Algebra 1” and “Algebra 2.” We will refrain from using these non-descriptive and unhelpful terms, given our new understanding of algebra — the more approriate name is classical algebra.

Classical algebra is the algebra of real numbers — the rules governing how real numbers and variables are manipulated. We should not underestimate the beauty and difficulty of classical algebra. Some problems, as we will see, are surprisingly difficult, even if they require applying familiar concepts.
