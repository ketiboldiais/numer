Problem Solving in Physics

The following problem-solving strategy is often useful for solving physics problems:

Identify explicit data. The first step is to identify all the relevant data. What are the problem's conditions? Given those conditions, what concepts apply?

Identify implicit data. Here, we want to identify any data that is implied from the explicit data. For example, if we know that ${x + 2 = 4,}$ we have the implicit data that ${x = 2.}$ Identifying these implicit data often proves enormously useful for later calculations. This is also the step where we identify things we don't know. What are the target variables? I.e., what are the things we want to find out about?

Set up the problem. This is where we want to layout all the equations, algorithms, systems, data structures, and concepts necessary to solving the problem. It may seem as if everything is relevant — with practice, this step can become much more like pattern-recognition.

Implement and execute the solution. Having laid out all of our tools, we now use them to implement a solution. Once implemented, we execute the solution and examine the results.

Test. Assuming the results returned correctly, we now test. Here, we'll want to replace the provided values with similar ones, then begin exploring edge cases.

Evaluate. Having performed all of the previous steps, we must then evaluate the answer. Think of this as writing a journal entry for the entire process, a sort of self-reflection. What is the significance for each step in the process? What are the solution's shortcomings? Were there edge cases that failed?

Modeling

One of the most valuable skills in physics is modeling — the process of reducing complex systems into smaller, simplified systems. For example, analyzing the motion of a bullet fired from a handgun is terrifyingly complex. How much gunpowder is used? What is the chemical composition of said gun powder? What is the handgun's model? Air resistance? Bullet's weight? Air temperature? Humidity? Distance? Height? All these questions are relevant, and unfortunately, paint a freakishly complex reality. If we tried solving the problem by answering all these questions at once, we likely wouldn't get anywhere.

Instead, a better approach is to model. Instead of thinking of the bullet with all its contours and metallic nature, we just envision a particle. Instead of worrying about the gun power chemistry, we just assume some initial acceleration. Instead of worrying about air resitance, we assume no air resistance; a vaccuum. We make whatever assumptions are necessary to make the system as easy as possible to analyze. This is modeling.

Units

In physics, measurements are a quantification of some physical quantity. Understanding how physical quantities relate begins with a measurement. And it is this understanding that leads to the laws of physics: Expressions of mathematical relationships among physical quantities. To understand these physical quantities and their relationships, we must properly interpret and analyze our measurements. And to do so, we need units of measurement. Accordingly, we present in this section the relevant units used in these materials, the quantities they measure, as well as the algebraic manipulations necessary for interpretation and analysis.

There are numerous units of measurement. For example, we have kilometers, milliseconds, centimeters, grams, micrograms, etc. We also have field-specific units. Astronomers use the unit ${\text{AU}}$ (astronomical units; the mean distance between the earth and the sun) and ${\text{ly}}$ (light-years; the distance light travels in vacuum in one Julian year — 365.25 days). Maritime workers are familiar with ${\text{nmi}}$ (the nautical mile). Bankers are familiar with dollars, euros, and yen, among many others. We also have system-specific units. In the customary system: inches, feet, yards, miles, pounds. In the Malagasi system: vari, nanki, sompi, bambou. And for the ancient Greeks: daktylos, pous, metretes.

Some units of measurement are easier to work with than others. It's fairly difficult to work with the customary system, in that the units appear arbitrarily grouped — ${12}$ inches to a foot, ${3}$ feet to a yard, ${1\space760}$ yards to a mile, ${3}$ miles to a league, ${16}$ ounces to a pound, ${2\space000}$ pounds to a ton. These are not conducive to easy mathematical manipulation. The SI system — where units are delimited via the decimal system — is far, far easier. Accordingly, this is the system we will use.

The quantity of length is the distance between two points in space. For example, an object at point ${p_i}$ and an object at point ${p_f}$ are separated by a length of ${\lvert p_f - p_i \rvert.}$

The quantity of mass is the amount of material composing an object (alternatively, how much the object resists change in its motion). Unlike length, mass is a property that inherently exists in an object. I.e., as long as the object exists, there is a mass. Contrast this with length, where we require an external reference (the other point) for the quantity's definition.

The quantity of time is what a clock reads after an established reference point. For example, if we start a clock at 00:00:00:000 (where each pair of zeros represents hours:minutes:seconds:centiseconds) and stop it at 00:00:01:067, we would have a time of ${1.067}$ seconds.

Uncertainty

Every measurement in physics must be provided with an explicit indication of uncertainty. Otherwise, the measurement is meaningless. This is because in physics (and in another science), we operation on the assumption that the true value of a measure exists. But, as a matter of logic and practice, we can never know that such a value exists. This stems from the fact that measurements are inherently restricted to three types of limitations:

  1. Instrumental limitations. All measuring devices are limited by how well they are made. Measure with a malfunctioning scale or poorly-designed measuring type, and our measurements are off.
  2. Systematic errors. Even if we had the best and most expensive measuring device in the world, failing to use the device properly can lead to errors. Alternatively, we might operate them properly but fail to conduct the experiment as planned. Importantly, these types of errors are not remedied by an explicit expression of uncertainty. The only remedy is to catch and eliminate them.
  3. Random errors. We can have the most well-designed measuring devices, operate them properly, and conduct the experiment correctly, but subtle variations in measurement technique and minute changes in the experimental environment can cause our measurements to be off.
