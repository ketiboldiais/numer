Combinatorics

Consider the following problem:

Problem

John plays a video game where there are 50 characters to chose from. John must construct a team of characters. The team can only consist of 5 unique characters, selected from the 50 characters. How many different teams can John construct?

This is an example of a combinatorics problem. Combinatorics is the study of combinations, or arrangements, of discrete objects. By and large, questions in combinatorics fall into a few categories:

  1. Existence. Given some things and a set of conditions, does a particular arrangement ${x}$ exist?
  2. Enumeration. How many different ways are there to arrange these things given this set of conditions?
  3. Analysis. What are the properties, or characteristics, of this particular arrangement?
  4. Optimization. Given all these different arrangements, what is the most optimal?

Not every combinatorics problem falls into the categories above, and of those that do, they may not fit so neatly. The categorization presented is intended to be illustrative, rather than comprehensive. However, many of the most useful ideas in combinatorics stem from the aforementioned questions, and it is these ideas that we explore. Accordingly, for our purposes, combinatorics is the study of the existence, enumeration, analysis, and optimization of arranging discrete objects.

Review of Induction

Before we examine more substantive material, it's worth reviewing mathematical induction, given how it forms the basis for many proofs in combinatorics.

A good illustration of induction is the sinking of the titanic. The titanic was a large cruise ship with bulkheads separated into sections.

Symbolically, we denote these sections as:

$$ (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, M, N, O, P) $$

The idea was that, if there were separate sections, if one or two would flood, the remaining would be left unflooded. When the titanic struck the ice berg, water flooded the sections towards the front:

$$ (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, M, N, O, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} P) $$

Unfortunately, the barriers between each of these sections weren't completely sealed, so water began leaking into the next sections:

$$ (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, M, N, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} O, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} P) $$

Then the next:

$$ (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, M, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} N, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} O, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} P) $$

And then the next:

$$ (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} M, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} N, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} O, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} P) $$

Eventually flooding all of the sections:

$$ \textcolor{cornflowerblue} {(A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, I, J, K, L, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} M, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} N, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} O, \textcolor{cornflowerblue} P)} $$

This illustrates the principle of induction. From the fact that the first ${n}$ sections flood, it follows that the ${n+1}$ sections will also flood.

Now that we know how to count multiples, we can start counting even more elaborate sequences: How many four-digit positive integers are perfect cubes?

Solution: We know that the first four-digit positive integer that is a perfect cube is ${10 \cdot 10 \cdot 10 = 1000.}$ The last four-digit perfect cube is a bit trickier to find. Fortunately, the method of exhaustion is feasible here. We know ${20 \cdot 20 \cdot 20 = 8000.}$ This is already pretty close to five-digits, so let's just try the next positive integer: ${21 \cdot 21 \cdot 21 = 9,261.}$ Now we're very close; 22 will probably enter five-digit territory: ${22 \cdot 22 \cdot 22 = 10,648.}$ Great, so we know our sequence is ${[1000, \ldots, 9261].}$ Taking the cubic root of each element, we have ${[10, \ldots, 21.]}$ Counting this list: ${(21 - 10) + 1 = 12.}$ Hence, there are 12 four-digit positive integers that are perfect cubes.

Hence, there are 12 possible paths for taking the route ${ABD.}$ In total, there are ${10 + 12 = 22}$ possible paths from ${A}$ to ${D.}$

The Product Rule

Consider the following problem:

Problem

A reality TV show features 4 women, each of whom are paired with 2 men. Only one woman and one of their paired men may be chosen as the most romantic couple. How many different outcomes are possible?

This is a basic counting problem. Suppose the women are Jane, Binita, Yuna, and Miremba. Jane is paired with: Remmy, Kento. Binita is paired with: Aiden, Jose. Yuna is paired with: Hans, Luka. And Miremba is paired with: Dan, Bo. Enumerating the different possible choices:

There are 8 possible outcomes. This corresponds to the following principle:

Product Rule.

Suppose two decisions, ${a}$ and ${b,}$ are made. If ${a}$ results in any one of ${m}$ possible outcomes, and if, for each outcome of ${a}$ there are ${n}$ possible outcomes of ${b,}$ then together there are ${m \cdot n}$ possible outcomes of the two decisions ${a}$ and ${b.}$

We can see that the rule above is true with a picture:

We can generalize the rule above for when there are more than 2 decisions made:

General Product Rule.

Suppose there are ${r_1 \ldots r_i}$ decisions to be performed, where ${r, i \in \Z^{+}.}$ Suppose further that ${r_1}$ results in ${n_1}$ possible outcomes, ${r_2}$ results in ${n_2}$ possible outcomes, ${r_3}$ in ${n_3}$ possible outcomes, ad infinitum. Then there is a total of ${n_1 \times n_2 \times n_3 \times \ldots \times n_r}$ possible outcomes.

Using the Sum & Product Rules Together

While the sum and product rules are useful tools for counting, they are limited. Often, a counting problem requires using both rules for a solution. For example, consider the following problem:

Problem

A computer language ${X}$ permits variable names according to the following rules:

  1. The variable name is a string of one or two alphanumeric characters (an alphanumeric character is a character that is either one of the 26 English letters or one of the 10 digits).
  2. Uppercase and lowercase letters are not distinguished.
  3. The variable name must begin with a letter.
  4. The variable name must not be one of the five strings of two characters reserved by the language.

How many different variable names are possible in ${X?}$

Solving this problem requires applying both the sum and product rules. First, let's denote the set of all possible variable names as ${V.}$ The set ${V}$ consists of two subsets: ${V_1,}$ the set of all possible variable names with a single character, and the set ${V_2,}$ the set of all possible variable names with two characters. The problem essentially asks us to determine ${n(V),}$ which is ${n(V_1) + n(V_2).}$

We first determine ${n(V_1).}$ Our immediate constraint is that the variable name must begin with a letter. Thus, for the first character, there are ${26}$ choices (uppercase and lowercase letters are not distinguished). This takes care of the first character and determines the number of possible variable names of a single character: ${26.}$

Next, we determine ${n(V_2).}$ The second character can be either one of the 26 English letters or one of the 10 digits. Definition: Combination

Given a set of size ${n,}$ choosing ${k}$ elements, the number of subsets with elements ${1, \ldots, k,}$ called combinations, is given by:

$$ C(n,k) = \dbinom{n}{k} = \dfrac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} $$

The Pigeonhole Principle

Say we had ${3}$ pigeons, and ${2}$ holes. Every pigeon must go into one, and only one, hole. The piegeonhole principle states that under these constraints, one hole must house at least two pigeons. This makes sense — there are more pigeons than holes, so one of the holes must be shared.

Pigeonhole Principle

Let ${n}$ and ${k}$ be positive integers, where ${k < n}$ Suppose ${n}$ identical objects must be placed into ${k}$ identical boxes. Then there will be at least one box in which we place at least two objects.

In pigeon-speak, given ${n}$ pigeons, each assigned to one of ${m}$ holes, if ${m < n,}$ then there must be some hole with at least ${2}$ pigeons.

Note what this theorem implies. If we had ${1000}$ pigeons and ${100}$ holes, at least one of those ${100}$ holes will hold at least ${2}$ pigeons. It doesn't state that some hole will house exactly ${2}$ pigeons, it merely states that some hole will house at least ${2}$ pigeons.

The theorem's proof is straightforward:

Proof: Pigeonhole Principle

Let ${n}$ and ${k}$ be positive integers, where ${k < n}$ Suppose ${n}$ objects must be placed into ${k}$ identical boxes. Now assume that no box contains at least two objects. It follows that each of the ${k}$ boxes contains either ${0}$ or ${1}$ object.

The pigeonhole principle seems simple, but it can lead to some surprising results. For example, from the pigeonhole principle, it follows that there is some pair of non-bald people in Chicago with exactly the same number of hairs on their head. How is this true? Well, there are about ${2.7 \times 10^6}$ people (pigeons) in Chicago. Each person has about ${10^5}$ hairs. Some people have only ${1}$ hair (a particular hole), others have ${2}$ hairs (another hole), and others have ${10^3}$ hairs (yet another hole). Thus, there are roughly ${10^5}$ possible holes. Because there are more people (pigeons) than holes (number of hairs), it follows that at least one pair of people have the exact same number of hairs on their head (share a hole).

Here's another example. A blue moon is a month where we have two full moons in the same month. This a fairly uncommon occurrence. The lunar cycle usually lasts a month, so most months have exactly one full moon. More specifically, a lunar cycle lasts roughly ${29.53}$ days, and every ${3}$ years, there are just a little over ${37}$ full lunar cycles. Thus, there are at least ${37}$ full moons in ${3}$ years. Now, in ${3}$ years, there are ${36}$ months. This means that by the pigeonhole principle, there is at least one blue moon in every ${3}$ year period. What this tells us is that, the saying Once in a blue moon does not denote something that occurs very rarely. Something occuring at least once every three years really isn't all that rare.

In general, to use the pigeonhole principle, we want to always answer the following questions before proceeding:

  • What are the pigeons? In other words, what are the objects we're working with?
  • What are the holes? By hole we mean some set. Importantly, we want to ensure that the holes are all distinct. The set must also be well-defined, in the sense that we can assign objects to it.
  • How are the pigeons assigned to the holes? We want to have some mechanism that can assign at least one object to one hole.
  • How do we know that there are more pigeons than holes? We must prove that there are more objects than there are sets.
