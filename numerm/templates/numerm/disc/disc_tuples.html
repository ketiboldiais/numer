Tuples

While $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} $, let $x_1, x_2, \ldots x_n$ be elements. An $n$-tuple consists of $x_1, x_2, \ldots x_n$, ordered as: first $x_1$, second $x_2$, third $x_3$, and so on, up to $x_n$.

When we describe things, we are not limited to ordered pairs. Things can obviously have many properties. For example: A weather model, at any given time, often consists of several properties: (latitude, longitude, temperature, pressure, wind speed, wind angle). A hitter in baseball has many properties: (batting average, home runs, runs batted in, runs scored, stolen bases). The federal government can be described as having three properties: (legislative, executive, judiciary). Each of those properties themselves might have properties — judiciary: (U.S. Supreme Court, Federal Courts of Appeals, Federal District Courts).

In mathematics, all of the above are called tuples — a finite ordered list (a sequence) of ordered elements. An $n$-tuple is a sequence of $n$ elements, where $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ (a non-negative integer). The name "tuple" is derived from the English nomenclature for multiple things: 2 for couple, 3 for triple, 4 for quadruple, etc. An ordered 2-tuple is an ordered pair. An ordered 3-tuple is an ordered triple.

An object's properties can often be the same as another object. For example:

  • most common elements in a mouse's body = (carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, phosphorus).
  • most common elements in a human's body = (carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, phosphorus).

The elements in the tuples above are said to be equal. We say that tuples are equal if, and only if, every element in the tuple is the same as the element with the same position in the other tuple(s).

An ordered 2-tuple is an ordered pair, and an ordered 3-tuple is an ordered triple. Two ordered $n$-tuples ($x_1, x_2, \ldots x_n$) and ($y_1, y_2, \ldots y_n$) are equal if, and only if, $x_1 = y_1$, $x_2 = y_2$, $\ldots$, $x_n = y_n$.

Cartesian Products

Symbolically, $A_1 \times A_2 \times \ldots \times A_n = \{ (a_1, a_2, \ldots , a_n) \mid a_1 \in A_1, a_2 \in A_2, \ldots , a_n \in A_n \}$ Particularly, $A_1 \times A_2 = \{ (a_1, a_2) \mid a_1 \in A_1, a_2 \in A_2 \}$ is the Cartesian product of $A_1$ and $A_2$.

Examples
  1. Let $A = \{ x, y \}$, $B = \{ 1, 2, 3 \}$, and $C = \{ a, b \}$.
    • What is $A \times B$?
      • $A \times B = \{ (x, 1), (y, 1), (x, 2), (y, 2), (x, 3), (y, 3) \}$.
    • What is $B \times A$?
      • $B \times A = \{ (1,x), (1,y), (2,x), (2,y), (3,x), (3,y) \}$.
    • What is $A \times A$?
      • $A \times A = \{ (x, x), (x, y), (y, x), (y, y) \}$.
    • How many elements are in $A \times B$?
      • There are 6 elements $A \times B$.
    • How many elements are in $B \times A$?
      • There are also 6 elements in $B \times A$.
    • How many elements are in $A \times A$?
      • There are 4 elements in $A$.
    • What is $(A \times B) \times C$?
      • $(A \times B) \times C = \{ ((x,1), a), ((x,1), b), \} $

${\R \times \R.}$ $\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$ is the Cartesian product — the set of all ordered pairs — $(x, y)$ where both $x$ and $y$ are real numbers. If we assign every $x$ to a point on a line and every $y$ to a point on another line perpendicular to $x$'s line, the lines form a plane. That plane is called the Cartesian plane, and every ordered pair $(x, y)$ lies on it.
