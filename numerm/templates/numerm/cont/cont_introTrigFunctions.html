Introduction: The Trigonometric Functions

Numerous processes are best described as iterating over regular intervals. The tides, progression from day to night, business cycles, these are all cyclical processes.

In mathematics, the periodic functions is the family of functions whose members repeat their values in regular intervals. The graph of a periodic function takes the general form of a pattern that keeps repeating. Because of this repetition, the functin's graph has the same output at exactly the same place in every cycle.

Because of the property of repetition, all the cycles of a periodic function have exactly the same length. This means all periodic functions have a helpful base case: The first cycle. If we know all the details of one full cycle of a periodic function, then we know all of the function's past and future outputs.

To begin understanding these functions, we first turn to a subset of the periodic functions — the trigonometric functions.
