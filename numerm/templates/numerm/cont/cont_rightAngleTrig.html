Special Triangles. Some angles serve as base cases because of how they cleanly divide the unit circle and how commonly they occur. These angles are ${0}$ (${{0}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 6}$ (${{30}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 4}$ (${{45}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 3}$ (${{60}^\circ}$), and ${\pi / 2}$ (${{90}^\circ}$).

The isosceles triangle, for example, is a right triangle with two equal sides and two equal angles. Because all right triangles must have a total degree measure of ${2 \pi,}$ we know that the isosceles triangle has two angles with a measure of ${\pi / 4,}$ and one angle, a right angle, of measure ${\pi / 2.}$ This means the isosceles triangle has lengths of ${s, s,}$ and ${\sqrt{2}s.}$

The 30-60-90 triangle is another special triangle with a right angle and two acute angles. In this case, the triangle has legs of lengths ${s}$ and ${\sqrt{3}s,}$ and a hypotenuse of ${2s.}$ The 30-60-90 triangle arises often because it is half of an equilateral triangle, a triangle with sides of all equal lengths. The 30-60-90 triangle's special nature comes from a theorem in the geometry kingdom:

Suppose ${y}$ is the length of the 30-60-90 triangle's leg opposite ${{60}^\circ.}$ Following the Pythagorean Theorem, we have ${y^2 + x^2 = (2x)^2,}$ where ${y}$ is the hypotenuse. In other words: ${y^2 = 4x^2 - x^2 = 3x^2.}$ Finally, solving for ${y}$ we have: ${y = \sqrt{3}x.}$

For the isosceles triangle, both legts are equal. Thus, we can label both legs with the same variable, ${x}$ (say). Hence, by the Pythagorean Theorem gain, ${y^2 = x^2 + x^2 = 2x^2,}$ where ${y}$ is the hypotenuse. Solving for ${y}$ we obtain ${y = \sqrt{2}x.}$

This is not a surprising result. The side opposite the angle ${\pi/3}$ is the side adjacent to the angle ${\pi/6,}$ so ${\sin (\pi/6)}$ and ${\cos (\pi/6)}$ are exactly the same ratio. The same goes for ${\cos (\pi/3)}$ and ${\sin (\pi/6).}$

The connection between sine and cosine extends to all the acute angles of a right triangle, given the facts that every right triangle has a total degree measure of ${\pi,}$ and because the two acute angles are complementary angles — because the right triangle has a measure of ${\pi/2,}$ the two acute angles must both total ${\pi/2.}$

The relationship between sine and cosine is the result of a cofunction identity — if any two angles are complementary, the sine of one is the cosine of the other, and vice versa. Knowing this relationship allows us to rewrite trigonometric expressions quickly.

Some of the more common cofunction identities:

${\sin \theta = \cos \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

${\cos \theta = \sin \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

${\sec \theta = \csc \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

${\csc \theta = \sec \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

${\tan \theta = \cot \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

${\cot \theta = \tan \left( \dfrac{\pi}{2} - \theta \right)}$

Notational Conventions. There are three notational conventions to bear in mind when handling the trigonometric functions. These conventions are largely a result of tradition and history.

First, we often suppress the parentheses for trigonometric functions, so long as doing so is not confusing. For example, ${sin (\theta)}$ is simply written as ${sin \theta.}$ To that end, if the function is parametrized with a particularly long expression, parentheses should be included. E.g., ${sin (\theta + 1/x).}$

Furthermore, parentheses are also suppressed for multiplication. For example, the expression ${\sin \theta \cos \theta}$ is equivalent to ${(\sin (\theta))(\cos (\theta)).}$ Similarly, ${2 \sin \theta}$ is equivalent to ${2(\sin (\theta)).}$ If we ever think that applying these conventions leads to ambiguity, it is best to err on the side of including parentheses.

Finally, the quantity ${(\sin \theta)^n}$ is almost always simply written as ${\sin^n \theta.}$ Thus, the expression ${\sin^n \theta}$ is equivalent to ${(\sin \theta)^2.}$ But this convention does not apply ${\sin^{-1} \theta.}$ This is an entirely different function (the ${\arcsin}$ function) to be addressed in later sections.

Applying Trigonometry

Knowing what we know now about trigonometry, we can now find find all three sides of a a triangle if we know the length of af at least one side and the measure of at least one acute angle.

For example, suppose a right triangle ${\triangle ABC,}$ with a hypotenuse of length ${x,}$ its longest leg of length ${y}$ and its shortest leg of length ${7.}$ ${\triangle ABC}$ is a 30-60-90 triangle. Why might be the lengths of ${a}$ and ${c?}$

First, because this is a 30-60-90 triangle, we know that ${\tan (\pi/6) = \dfrac{7}{a}.}$ We can then write ${a = \dfrac{7}{\tan(\pi/6)} \approx 12.1244.}$ Next, we can then use sine to find the hypotenuse. ${sin(\pi/6) = \dfrac{7}{c}.}$ Rewriting to solve for ${c,}$ ${c = \dfrac{7}{\sin(\pi/6)} = 14.}$
