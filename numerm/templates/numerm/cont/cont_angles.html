Angles

A ray is one point on a line and all points extending in one direction from that point. If we have two rays sharing a single point from which they extend, then we have an angle. This shared endpoint is called the angle's vertex. The two rays are called the angle's sides. For example, consider the angle below:

We denote the angle above with the symbol ${\angle ABC.}$ The individual sides of ${\angle ABC}$ are referenced by referring to the rays themselves, ${\overrightarrow{\rm BA}}$ and ${\overrightarrow{\rm BC.}}$

The creation of an angle is a dynamic procedure. First, the initial position — two rays lying on top of each other. We can refer to this first step as the base case of angle creation; an angle of ${0^\circ.}$ One of the two rays lies fixed, the other is free to move. The fixed ray is called the angle's initial side, and the free ray is called the angle's terminal side.

To identify the different sides, we draw a small arc and arrow close to the vertex, indicating the direction and distance the terminal side moves:

The angle's measure is the amount of rotation from the initial side to the terminal side. This amount is contextualized with a unit of measurement. The two common units for measuring angles are degrees, and radians.

Geometry v. Trigonometry. A triangle ${\triangle ABC}$ has sides of length 8, 8, and 8. What are the measures of its angles?

Solution

Because ${\triangle ABC}$ has three equal sides, its three angles are also equal. Since the sum of the angles is ${180^\circ,}$ each angle has a measure of ${180^\circ / 3 = 60^\circ.}$

Geometry allows us to complete the simple problem above. But suppose ${\triangle DEF}$ has sides 5, 6, and 7. What are the measures of ${\triangle DEF's}$ angles? What about ${\triangle GHI,}$ where ${\overrightarrow{\rm HG}}$ has a length of 3, ${\overrightarrow{\rm HI}}$ has a length of 4, and the angle between them is ${90^\circ.}$ What are measures of its other angles? These sorts of questions cannot be answered with simple geometry. Instead, what we need is trigonometry.

Coterminal Angles. Recall that negative angles are angles measured clockwise from the terminal side. While negative angles are sometimes necessary, they can be awkward to work with, and it is often best to use the corresponding coterminal angle whenever possible. Given an angles ${\angle BAC,}$ and ${\angle CAD,}$ both angles are coterminal if and only if both ${\angle BAC}$ and ${\angle CAD}$ share the same side.

From the diagram above, an angle measure of ${\pi / 2,}$ ${{90}^\circ,}$ is coterminal with an angle of measure ${3 \pi / 2,}$ or ${{270}^\circ.}$ When thinking about coterminal angles, we must consider the reference angle. The reference angle is the measure of the smallest, positive, acute angle ${\theta}$ formed by the terminal side of the angle ${\theta}$ and the horizontal axis. From this definition, a positive reference angle has a terminal side in the first quadrant.
