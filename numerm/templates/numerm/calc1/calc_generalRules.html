Derivative Formulas

We can think of derivative formulas as falling into two categories: (1) the specific, and (2) the general. A specific formula provides a way to differentiate a specific example. For example, ${f(x) = x^n}$ has the derivative ${f'(x) = nx^{n-1}.}$

In contrast, general formulas are those that apply to all functions. For example:

In the first formula above: The derivative of the sum of two functions is the sum of the derivatives of the two functions. And in the second: The derivative of a constant multiplied by a function is the constant multiplied by the derivative of the function. Both specific and general formulas are needed for polynomials.

The Product Rule

It may be tempting to think that the derivative of a product is the product of the derivatives. This is wrong, and we've already seen why. Given the function ${f(x) = x^2,}$ the derivative of ${f}$ is ${f'(x) = 2x.}$ However, the function ${f(x) = x^2}$ can be written as ${f(x) = x \cdot x.}$ If it were the case the derivative of a product is the product of the derivatives, then we would have ${\frac{d}{dx} (x^2) = \frac{d}{dx}(x) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(x) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1.}$ Wrong.
