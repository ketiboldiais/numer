Differentiating Exponential & Logarithmic Functions

We now turn to differentiating exponential and logarithmic functions. Knowing how to compute the derivatives for these functions is especially useful in applied mathematics fields. For example, we frequently see exponential functions with future value calculations in finance, and logarithmic functions for discounting to present value. We also see exponential functions in population growth models. In all of these situations, knowing how to compute various rates of change is invaluabe. And as we've seen, that knowledge is provided through the derivative.

We begin by recalling some of the basic rules of exponents. First, where ${a, b > 0,}$ the following rules hold:

Name Rule Remark Constraints
Exponent-product Rule ${\large a^{x_1} \cdot a^{x_2} = a^{x_1 + x_2}}$ The exponential of the sum is the exponential of the product. ${a \in \Z^{+},}$ ${x_1, x_2 \in \Z}$
Exponent-quotient Rule ${\large \dfrac{a^{x_1}}{a^{x_2}} = a^{x_1 - x_2} = \dfrac{1}{a^{x_2 - x_1}}}$ If the bases are the same, keep the base and subtract the exponent in the denominator from the exponent in the numerator. ${a \in \Z^{+},}$ ${x_1, x_2 \in \Z}$
Raising a Product to a Power ${\large (ab)^{x} = a^xb^x}$ To raise a product to the ${n^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ power, raise each factor to the ${n^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ power. ${a, b \in \Z^{+},}$ ${x \in \Z}$
Exponent-power Rule ${\large (a^{x_1})^{x_2} = a^{x_1 \cdot x_2}}$ To raise a power to a power, multiply the exponents. ${a, x_1, x_2 \in \Z^{+}}$
Raising a Quotient to a Power ${\large \left(\dfrac{a}{b}\right)^{x} = \dfrac{a^x}{b^x}}$ To raise a quotient to the ${n^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ power, raise both the numerator and the denominator to the ${n^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ power. ${a, x \in \Z^{+}}$
Exponent of 1 ${\large a^1 = a}$ Any number raised to the first power returns the original number. ${a \in \R}$
Exponent of 0 ${\large a^0 = 1}$ Any number raised to the zero power returns 1. ${\{a \in \R : a \neq 0\};}$ ${0^0 = \text{undefined}}$
Negative Integer Exponent Rule ${\large a^{-x} = \dfrac{1}{a^x}}$ Negative exponents are equivalent to an exponent in the denominator. ${a, x \in \Z^{+}}$
Fractional Exponent Rule ${\large a^{\frac{x_1}{x_2}} = \sqrt[x_2]{a^{x_1}}}$ Fractional exponents are equivalent to the ${\text{denom}^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ root of the ${\text{numer}^{\text{\scriptsize{th}}}}$ power. ${a, x_1, x_2 \in \Z^{+}}$
