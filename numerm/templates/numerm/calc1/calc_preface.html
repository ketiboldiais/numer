Single-variable Calculus

Let ${\epsilon < 0 \ldots}$

The materials that follow explore single-variable calculus. Included in this volume are materials that would fall under a course called “Precalculus” and “Calculus I” in the American education system. We refrain from using this distinction for several reasons: (1) It denotes that there is some distinction between the materials covered when there is not. The materials covered in a “precalculus” course are the foundations for “calculus”, not separate. (2) With an online format, it is easier to keep things flowing; we do not have to deal with the constraints of physical publishing. This is all to say that we don't recognize “precalculus” as some distinct course or field that merits its own volume. It's the beginning of calculus, and it should be taken seriously.

Calculus is an enormously important field. It is the study of smooth changes, or continuity. It is the base language of physics, and is employed in biology, chemistry, finance, some fields of computer science, and many, many more. The pervasiveness of calculus partly because a great many things in nature are continuous. Accordingly, the kingdom of calculus falls under the realm of continuous mathematics.

One way to distinguish between continuous mathematics and discrete mathematics (e.g., graph theory, combinatorics, set theory, discrete probability, etc.) is the difference between analog and digital watches. In an analog watch, there are infinitely many times between 1:00P.M. and 1:01P.M. More generally, all along the analog watch's face, there are infinitely many times. In a digital watch, however, there is a discrete amount of time between 1:00P.M. and 1:01P.M. This is because a digital watch can only count up to a certain decimal place without causing error.

The need to study calculus cannot be understated. The following materials conduct a rigorous investigation of ideas in calculus. To ensure we do so succinctly and efficiently, we assume the reader has taken a basic discrete and continuous mathematics course.
