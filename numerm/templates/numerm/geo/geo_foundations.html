Geometry: Foundations

We begin by introducing the foundations of classical geometry. These foundations underlie all of the axioms, lemmas, theorems, and corollaries to come. As one of the oldest fields, some of these foundational propositions appear overly simplistic, trivial, or elementary. And indeed, they often are. Classical geometry's beauty, however, sprouts from these humble seedlings — from the trivial and mundane erupts awe and peculiarity. Recognizing this beauty, we will steadily appreciate why early mathematicians equated the field with mysticism and the divine.

Points

The fundamental geometric object is the point. It's often depicted as a small black dot, but in geometry, a point has no size. It has a location, but no length, width, height, depth, or any other property we attribute to “size.”. It only has a location. sets of points form geometric objects. Or, put another way, geometric objects are sets of points.

When an infinite number of points follow a straight path, we have a line. Tying back to the set theory interpretation, a line is an infinite set of points, and we denote the set by picking two arbitrary points on the line, name them (say, ${A}$ and ${B}$), and write: ${\overleftrightarrow{\rm AB.}}$ Alternatively, we can denote the line with single variable, such as ${\ell.}$

When an infinite number of points follows a straight path from a discrete point (i.e., a particular location), we have a ray. Like the line, a ray is an infinite set of points, but with the added feature that it has a starting point. Also like the line, we denote a ray by naming the starting point (say ${A}$) and some arbitrary point on the line (say ${B}$), and writing: ${\overrightarrow{\rm AB.}}$

When we have infinitely many points between one discrete point, call it ${A,}$ and another discrete point, call it ${B,}$ we have a line segment, denoted ${\overline{\rm AB.}}$ The points ${A}$ and ${B}$ are called endpoints, of ${\overline{\rm AB.}}$

Angles

An angle is a geometric object formed by two rays originating in the same endpoint. The two rays are called the sides of the angle, and their common endpoint is called the vertex of the angle.
