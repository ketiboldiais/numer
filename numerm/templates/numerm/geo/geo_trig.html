Trigonometry

Angles

A ray is one point on a line and all points extending in one direction from that point. If we have two rays sharing a single point from which they extend, then we have an angle. This shared endpoint is called the angle's vertex. The two rays are called the angle's sides. For example, consider the angle below:

We denote the angle above with the symbol ${\angle ABC.}$ The individual sides of ${\angle ABC}$ are referenced by referring to the rays themselves, ${\overrightarrow{\rm BA}}$ and ${\overrightarrow{\rm BC.}}$

The creation of an angle is a dynamic procedure. First, the initial position — two rays lying on top of each other. We can refer to this first step as the base case of angle creation; an angle of ${0^\circ.}$ One of the two rays lies fixed, the other is free to move. The fixed ray is called the angle's initial side, and the free ray is called the angle's terminal side.

To identify the different sides, we draw a small arc and arrow close to the vertex, indicating the direction and distance the terminal side moves:

The angle's measure is the amount of rotation from the initial side to the terminal side. This amount is contextualized with a unit of measurement. The two common units for measuring angles are degrees, and radians.

Geometry v. Trigonometry. A triangle ${\triangle ABC}$ has sides of length 8, 8, and 8. What are the measures of its angles? Because ${\triangle ABC}$ has three equal sides, its three angles are also equal. Since the sum of the angles is ${180^\circ,}$ each angle has a measure of ${180^\circ / 3 = 60^\circ.}$

Geometry allows us to complete the simple problem above. But suppose ${\triangle DEF}$ has sides 5, 6, and 7. What are the measures of ${\triangle DEF's}$ angles? What about ${\triangle GHI,}$ where ${\overrightarrow{\rm HG}}$ has a length of 3, ${\overrightarrow{\rm HI}}$ has a length of 4, and the angle between them is ${90^\circ.}$ What are measures of its other angles? Radians v. Degrees. A unit degree is a ratio. More specifically, ${{1}^\circ = 1/360.}$ The constant ${{360}^\circ}$ constants a full circular rotation. Why 360? Purely arbitrary. It so happens that 360 divides nicely, and works well with time measures (i.e., 60 seconds to a minute; 60 seconds to an hour; in ancient calendars, there were 360 days in a year; and the ancient Babylonians had a base-60 number system — in essence, more than likely a cultural artifact).

Under the degree unit, a circle is defined to have ${{360}^\circ.}$ Thus, a perfect horizontal angle (a straight angle) has a measure of ${{180}^\circ.}$ A right angle has a measure of ${{90}^\circ.}$ An angle with a measure less than ${{90}^\circ}$ is called an acute angle, and an angle with a measure greater than ${{90}^\circ}$ is an obtuse angle.

If we measure an angle counterclockwise from the initial side, the angle is called a positive angle, and has a positive number measure. Inversely, if we measure an angle clockwise from the initial side, the angle is called a negative angle, and has a negative number measure. Angles whose terminal side lies on an axis — those with measures ${{0}^\circ,}$ ${{90}^\circ,}$ ${{270}^\circ,}$ and ${{360}^\circ}$ — are called quadrantal angles.

Degrees are what most non-mathematicians are familiar with, and is the unit we most often encounter at a hardware store. Degrees, however, are not an ideal unit when handling trigonometric functions and equations in higher-level mathematics. Instead, radians are what mathematicians most often use. This is because when degrees are used as units in calculus and other more complex fields, they almost always end up being converted to radians, then back to degrees. This is an unnecessary computation since we could have just started with radians in the first place.

Radians can be easily converted to degrees. From the diagram above, we know that ${2 \pi \text{ rad} = {360}^\circ,}$ ${\dfrac{\pi}{2} \text{ rad} = {90}^\circ,}$ ${\dfrac{\pi}{4} \text{ rad} = {45}^\circ,}$ etc. Because a radian is simply a ratio of two lengths (the radius and the arc,) we need not include the unit “rad.” When it is included, it is simply used to indicate an angle measurement. Accordingly, we can simply write ${2 \pi,}$ ${\dfrac{\pi}{4},}$ and so on. The variable ${r}$ is actually a constant, since all angle measures using radians can be expressed as the measure of an angle on the unit circle, a circle with radius of length 1. Accordingly, we can simply omit the constant ${r}$ when using radians. It is precisely because of this property that mathematicians prefer the radius — computations and algebraic manipulations are much, much cleaner.

Special Triangles. Some angles serve as base cases because of how they cleanly divide the unit circle and how commonly they occur. These angles are ${0}$ (${{0}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 6}$ (${{30}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 4}$ (${{45}^\circ}$), ${\pi / 3}$ (${{60}^\circ}$), and ${\pi / 2}$ (${{90}^\circ}$).

The isosceles triangle, for example, is a right triangle with two equal sides and two equal angles. Because all right triangles must have a total degree measure of ${2 \pi,}$ we know that the isosceles triangle has two angles with a measure of ${\pi / 4,}$ and one angle, a right angle, of measure ${\pi / 2.}$ This means the isosceles triangle has lengths of ${s, s,}$ and ${\sqrt{2}s.}$

The 30-60-90 triangle is another special triangle with a right angle and two acute angles. In this case, the triangle has legs of lengths ${s}$ and ${\sqrt{3}s,}$ and a hypotenuse of ${2s.}$ The 30-60-90 triangle arises often because it is half of an equilateral triangle, a triangle with sides of all equal lengths. Suppose ${y}$ is the length of the 30-60-90 triangle's leg opposite ${{60}^\circ.}$ Following the Pythagorean Theorem, we have ${y^2 + x^2 = (2x)^2,}$ where ${y}$ is the hypotenuse. In other words: ${y^2 = 4x^2 - x^2 = 3x^2.}$ Finally, solving for ${y}$ we have: ${y = \sqrt{3}x.}$

For the isosceles triangle, both legts are equal. Thus, we can label both legs with the same variable, ${x}$ (say). Hence, by the Pythagorean Theorem gain, ${y^2 = x^2 + x^2 = 2x^2,}$ where ${y}$ is the hypotenuse. Solving for ${y}$ we obtain ${y = \sqrt{2}x.}$

The connection between sine and cosine extends to all the acute angles of a right triangle, given the facts that every right triangle has a total degree measure of ${\pi,}$ and because the two acute angles are complementary angles — because the right triangle has a measure of ${\pi/2,}$ the two acute angles must both total ${\pi/2.}$

The relationship between sine and cosine is the result of a cofunction identity — if any two angles are complementary, the sine of one is the cosine of the other, and vice versa. Knowing this relationship allows us to rewrite trigonometric expressions quickly.

Notational Conventions. There are three notational conventions to bear in mind when handling the trigonometric functions. These conventions are largely a result of tradition and history.

First, we often suppress the parentheses for trigonometric functions, so long as doing so is not confusing. For example, ${sin (\theta)}$ is simply written as ${sin \theta.}$ To that end, if the function is parametrized with a particularly long expression, parentheses should be included. E.g., ${sin (\theta + 1/x).}$

Furthermore, parentheses are also suppressed for multiplication. For example, the expression ${\sin \theta \cos \theta}$ is equivalent to ${(\sin (\theta))(\cos (\theta)).}$ Similarly, ${2 \sin \theta}$ is equivalent to ${2(\sin (\theta)).}$ If we ever think that applying these conventions leads to ambiguity, it is best to err on the side of including parentheses.

Finally, the quantity ${(\sin \theta)^n}$ is almost always simply written as ${\sin^n \theta.}$ Thus, the expression ${\sin^n \theta}$ is equivalent to ${(\sin \theta)^2.}$ But this convention does not apply ${\sin^{-1} \theta.}$ This is an entirely different function (the ${\arcsin}$ function) to be addressed in later sections.

Applying Trigonometry

Knowing what we know now about trigonometry, we can now find find all three sides of a a triangle if we know the length of af at least one side and the measure of at least one acute angle.

For example, suppose a right triangle ${\triangle ABC,}$ with a hypotenuse of length ${x,}$ its longest leg of length ${y}$ and its shortest leg of length ${7.}$ ${\triangle ABC}$ is a 30-60-90 triangle. Why might be the lengths of ${a}$ and ${c?}$

First, because this is a 30-60-90 triangle, we know that ${\tan (\pi/6) = \dfrac{7}{a}.}$ We can then write ${a = \dfrac{7}{\tan(\pi/6)} \approx 12.1244.}$ Next, we can then use sine to find the hypotenuse. ${sin(\pi/6) = \dfrac{7}{c}.}$ Rewriting to solve for ${c,}$ ${c = \dfrac{7}{\sin(\pi/6)} = 14.}$
