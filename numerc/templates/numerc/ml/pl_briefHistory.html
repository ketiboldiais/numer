A Brief History of Programming Languages

In the beginning, programs were sequences of movements, carried out by mechanical devices. One of the earliest examples was the difference engine, a mechanical calculator invented by the English engineer Charles Babbage in the 1820s. The difference engine was designed to tabulate polynomial functions, a critically important task for engineering, scientific analysis, and navigation.

Several years later, in 1837, Babbage proposed a more general-purpose computer, the Analytical Engine, as a successor to his earlier work. Compared to the difference engine, the Analytical Engine was much more sophisticated — it had an arithmetic logic unit, conditional branching and loops, as well as integrated memory. Babbage never saw his idea to fruition, but if he did, it would've looked and operated uncannily similar to modern computers.

Babbage's ideas were partly inspired by the Jacquard Loom, a loom invented by the French weaver Joseph Marie Jacquard in 1804. Jacquard created the loom as way to simplify the manufacture of textiles with complex patterns. This was accomplished by feeding the loom a chain of punchcards. The punchcards contained the instructions for the patterns, and when fed to the machine, the card would either push, or not push (given a hole), small levers. Arranging the holes on the punchcard, we can imagine how the resulting arrangement of moved and unmoved levers can lead to quick pattern construction. This use of punchcards not only inspired Babbage, but computers over a century later.

Following Babbage's working, a variety of punched-card-handling machines appeared. Many of these early computers required manually plugging and unplugging various wires and components for computations.

It was after the Second World War that we began seeing the proliferation of much more sophisticated computers. The ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer) was the first programmable digital computer. Financed by the United States Army, the computer was intended to analyze ballistics results for the United States Army's Ballistic Research Laboratory. It consisted of over 17,000 vacuum tubes, weighed 30 tons, and consumed 140 kW for its operations.

As mathematics and computer science progressed, researchers and academics began looking at programs differently — programs are data. This shift led to the idea of of encoding programs as numbers, and storing those numbers as data. I.e., machine language.
