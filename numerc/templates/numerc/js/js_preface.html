Event-driven Programming: JavaScript

The name “JavaScript” is a product of history and marketing. JavaScript was originally referred to by its developers as “Mocha,” but because the language was created to entice Java developers, the marketing experts concluded that JavaScript was a better fit, as “script” was the word du jour for “easy of use.” While there are some similarities between Java and JavaScript, they are two completely different and unrelated languages—Java is to JavaScript what ham is to hamster.

JavaScript is a dynamically- and weakly-typed programming language. Thus, unlike languages like C++ and Java, JavaScript is compiled and executed at runtime. Undoubtedly, that execution most commonly occurs as part of a webpage in a browser, but thanks to various JavaScript libraries, we can execute also JavaScript directly on a machine through a host environment. To begin understanding why JavaScript exists and how it works, we should talk about the internet from a very high-level overview.

Networks

The word “internet” was used as early as 1974 as shorthand for “internetwork,” likely in reference to the notion of interconnected computer networks. Accordingly, to study what the internet is, we should have basic understanding of computer networks.

We might have some vague understanding of a computer network:

The grey point in the center is the mystery. What exactly happens between the client (i.e., a device) and the server? Our goal is to try and reveal what's behind the grey point.

Computer networks is a broad field, consisting of three sub-fields: (1) communications, (2) networks, and (3) distributed systems. Communications is the lowest level, providing the theory and foundations for all modern computer systems. It's the study of how we use signals, 1s and 0s, to communicate information. Communications is traditionally handled by electrical engineers, physicists, and chemists, as most of the applications at such a low level concern electronics and materials science.

Networks are abstractions of communications. At this level, we focus on the software: How the information is represented, packaged, and transmitted; the rules governing information transmission; optimization and efficiency in constructing networking systems.

At the highest level are distributed systems. Distributed systems is the study of how to create, improve, and maintain computing environments whose components are spread across multiple computers on a network. Such environments include peer-to-peer applications like uTorrent and the historic Limewire; massive multiplayer online games like World of War Craft and Guild Wars; wireless sensor networks; aircraft control systems; and increasingly important in the modern era, parallel computation.

Our predominant focus will be on networks, but we will occasionally touch on communications and distributed networks for a better picture of how everything fits together.

Network Uses

Today, so much of our lives depends on networks. Email, wireless printers, streaming news, movies, video clips, messaging, transferring finances, phone calling, computer gaming, maps, securing information, accessing information, signing contracts, ordering takeout and groceries — none of this is possible without networks. Let's go over some of the most important use cases for networks.

Communication. One of the most important uses for networks is communication. VoIP (“voice-over-IP”), video conferencing, instant messaging, and social networking are all examples of using networks for communication. A core goal in communication networks is achieving low latency, high speed. Communication networks close to this goal are often characterized as “fast” (i.e., “high-speed internet”). Latency and bandwidth, however, are two very different concepts.

Bandwidth is what many perceive as a network's “speed.” It's a measure of how much data can be transferred from one point in a network to another. High bandwidth, however, doesn't imply “high speed.” We can think of bandwidth as akin to a freeway. Say you're downloading a 600 Mb (Megabyte) file. If the bandwidth was, say, 1000 Mbps (megabits per second), it would take you about 4.8 seconds to download the file (there are 4800 megabits in 600 megabytes). Following the freeway analogy, we can think of each megabit as piece of data, and at 1000 Mbps, we have a thousand lanes open on the freeway. If there are too many people on the network (i.e., congestion), or the interent service provider places a speed limit, some of those lanes may be closed. With enough congestion or a cap, that 1000 Mbps could be brought down to 100Mbps, in which case it would take 48 seconds to download the file.

Higher bandwidth, however, doesn't necessarily mean “high-speed.” There is always the problem of latency — how much time it takes for a signal to travel to its destination and back. We can think of latency as how long it takes to get a response after stating a request. For example, you might be at a drive through, and there's no one in line. You get to the intercom, the teller greets you, and you state your order. A second goes by, no response. A minute goes by, no response. At 2 to 3 minutes, you're likely starting to get irritated. Any longer than that, and you just drive away. There was no one in line and you got to the intercom fine, but it wasn't “fast.” You sat there for quite some time! The same idea extends to latency.

Resource Sharing. In addition to communication, networks are critical for resource sharing. The need to share resources traces its origins all the way back to the ancient days of mainframe computers, where timesharing was the norm. Today, that need continues in various media. At a large company office, for example, it's unlikely that every worker has their own printer. Instead, there are likely several printers in a particular location that workers can send tasks to. Resource sharing extends beyond the printer. Cloud-based tools like online file converters and video downloaders are all examples of resource sharing. As resources grow increasingly costly, resource-sharing is a tremendously cost-effective way of meeting demand.

The Internet

The internet is the penultimate example of a human accomplishment from humble beginnings. The internet traces its origins to 1969, with a small, tiny network called ARPANET (The U.S. “Advanced Research Projects Agency Network”). Before 1970 (when ARPANET was implemented), communicating between computers (large, unwieldy mainframe systems at the time) was done with circuit switching, much like how telephones were connected, end to end, with wired connections.

While this system worked, they were slow and easily broke down. Two actors fanned the flames for optimization: At the height of the Cold War, military officials wanted connections that could continue working in the event of a nuclear strike. Separately, researchers at various universities across the United States wanted more reliable connections for sharing data and computational resources.

In 1962, Paul Baran — a computer scientist at the Rand Corporation — proposed that data be broken down into smaller pieces, and sent along different paths to a destination. Novel as it was, critics dismissed Baran's idea, and it fell short of implementation. As we'll see later, Baran's idea now forms the underlying basis for the World Wide Web.

Several years later, the director of the U.S. Department of Defense's Advanced Research Project Agency (ARPA), J.C.R. Licklider — known by his colleagues as “Lick” — began outlining the idea of a massive “Intergalactic Computer Network.” Following ARPA's tradition of leaving after a year or two to give others a chance at management, Licklider returned to academia before seeing his idea to fruition. Licklider, however, took care in ensuring Robert Taylor and Irvin Sutherland, two highly-accomplished researchers at ARPA, continued his vision. By December of 1969, Sutherland and Taylor established an experimental network connection between UC Santa Barbara, UCLA, the Stanford Research Institution, and the University of Utah School of Computing. Several months later, in July 1970, the network expanded to include MIT and the Rand Corporation, among others. And by March 1971, the network included the University of Illinois, Harvard, and several other locations, connecting 15 computers spread from the West to the East Coast. That experimental network, ARPANET, would later become the internet, to which well over 10 billion devices are now connected.

Wired Connections

In the modern era, we've gotten used to everything being wireless. This author fondly remembers the days of dial up. With smartphones growing more and more pervasive, we no longer ask if a hotel room provides in-unit ethernet cables. In fact, we don't even ask questions at all — we just assume the business provides WiFi, and if that isn't the case, that there's service for LTE usage.

The shocker: On the internet, most data is not moved on a wireless connection. In fact, wireless connections are at the very fringe of the internet. What do we mean by this? Imagine the interent as akin to the Tokyo train lines. There are numerous stops; the wireless connection is among the very last (if not the last). The vast majority of data transmitted via the internet is moved via fiber optic cables. Wireless connections are simply too slow, too small, and have too little range to transmit the voluminous amounts of data we supply and demand.

The fiber optic cables supporting the internet lay across land and sea. They go through the oceans, passing from country to country, and within the countries, pass from state to state (or provinces/prefectures/etc.), city to city, and so on. The data we obtain and transmit wirelessly is actually received from, and sent to, a tower, which is itself connected to some juncture along the myriad of cables. In the United States, one very interesting aspect of these fiber optic cables is that they usually run along the federal interstate highways. These highways themselves trace their origins to railroad lines from the 19th Century. Why do these connections exist? It's a lot easier to maintain things and fix problems on large infrastructures if we use pre-existing paths to traverse the infrastructure.

For example, suppose we're on our home WiFi network, and want to see some lecture notes, contained in a PDF file, from a course offered at the University of Adelaide in Australia. Then suppose that those notes were stored on a server in Australia. When click on the link to that PDF, we are sending a request for those notes (more on this later), and the server responds with the notes. We do not send that request or receive the response with only a wireless connection. That would be far, far too slow. What instead happens is that the request is sent from our computer to our router, then from the router to some closer server, then from there to a tower, then from the tower all the way through the fiber optic cables across land and sea to that server in Australia. The truly remarkable aspect of all this is that depending on our internet connection and how large the PDF file is, this all occurs in a second or less. Even more remarkable: Countries have agreed to how these fiber optics cables transmit data, whether they have good or bad diplomatic relations.

How do these fiber optic cables work so quickly? Through light and glass. Data is transmitted across these cables in the form of light, bouncing off the glass from point to point. Given that light travels at the fastest speed known to man (roughly 300,000 kilometers per second or 186,000 miles per second) — it's no surprise that data transmission along the cables is swift.

At the very edges of these fiber optic cables, there are copper-based connections. For example, in a large building, there is likely a wall socket connecting some router, or multiple routers. Each router provides a wireless connection for a certain area (recall how walking away from a certain area reduces the strength of a wireless connection). The router's connections are copper-based, not fiber optic. For particularly large buildings, the router connections might lead to some obscure closet containing a hub for all the routers. Behind that hub, there's likely a few slender fiber optic cables leading some electrical tower nearby, which itself connects to other junctions along the fiber optic cable network. These shorter copper-based connections are used primarily because copper is cheaper than glass fiber. However, we do not use copper for long-range connections because electrical signals along copper generally only have a range of 100 meters. Beyond 100 meters, these signals begin degrading.

In recent years, numerous companies have jumped in on the fiber-optic cable market. In various suburban areas, we might notice signs saying a particular company offers fiber-optic connections in the area. These companies essentially lay fiber-optic connections from homes (usually much smaller buildings, and as such, rely on slower copper connections) to a nearby tower. What may be perplexing is that these areas appear almost arbitrary — one side of the neighborhood has access to a connection, while the other does not. This is a result of how expensive fiber-optic connections are. Companies will provide these connections only if there's sufficient interest to warrant the cost of laying down a cable. It would be a poor investment to lay a fiber optic cable for a single home.

Wireless Connections

In the modern era, wireless connections are almost always our first connection to the internet. Wireless connections come in various forms, one of which is a Wifi network. In a large building or room, we might notice medium-sized boxes with blinking lights (and potentially an antenna). These are routers, and they provide a Wifi connection. As mentioned earlier, these routers have copper based connections to some hub elsewhere. Wifi is what we use for short-range connections, and they primarily set up for personal use.

Another form of wireless connection is through a mobile data network. Instead of a router, mobile data networks are provided by large cell towers. If we've ever wondered why cell phone plans cost so much, one contributing factor is the cost of maintaining and improving the mobile data networks provided by the cell towers.

How Do Devices Communicate on the Internet?

We've established a broad overview of the internet's underlying infastructure, but now we have another question: How do devices connected to the internet know when to send and receive data? In other words, how do these devices communciate?

The answer this question, we add another layer of abstraction to the internet: The internet is not just a physical infrastructure; it's also a communication protocol — agreement as to how two or more entities exchange data. The Internet Protocol (IP) is series of agreements that allow internet-connected devices to communicate. IP specifies the format of IP addresses — the identifiers, or names, for devices connected to the internet. IP has underwent several changes to the way IP addresses are formatted:

The IP address above is an IPv4 (read “IP version 4”) address, formatted in dotted decimal notation. Under IPv4, IP addresses consisted of 32 bits. With 32 bits, we can generate ${2^{32},}$ roughly 4.3 billion, unique addresses. The problem: There are now more than 4.3 billion people on Earth, many of whom have multiple devices, not counting all of the devices that aren't directly used by people. Because of this problem, we are now on IPv6 (IPv5 was proposed but never formally adopted):

With IPv6, addresses went from 32 bits to 128 bits. With 128 bits, we generate ${2^{128}}$ unique addresses, roughly ${3.4 \times 10^{38},}$ or 340 undecillion. This is probably big enough. Fun aside: ${2^{32}}$ is roughly 4.3 billion, ${2^{64}}$ is about 18.4 quintillion ${(\approx 1.8 \times 10^{19})}$, ${2^{128}}$ about 340 undecillion ${(\approx 3.4 \times 10^{38})}$, ${2^{256}}$ is roughly 115 quattuorvigintillion ${(\approx 1.158 \times 10^{77}),}$ ${2^{512}}$ is about ${1.34 \times 10^{154}}$ (Latin has its limits), ${2^{1024}}$ is about ${1.798 \times 10^{308},}$ and ${2^{2056}}$ is about ${8.27 \times 10^{618}.}$

In addition to the naming rule, IP also specifies how messages should be formatted when they're exchanged across the internet. We can think of this as akin to the local post office's rules on what we can and cannot put into certain boxes or envelopes. We can place whatever we want in the box or envelope (subject to criminal sanctions), but for the box or envelope to be sent, we have to follow the carrier's rules. In IP terms, that box or envelope is called a datagram.

The datagram consists of several fields. For our purposes, the three most important fields are the source IP address, destination IP address, and data field. As we can guess, the the source IP address indicates where the datagram originates, and the destination IP address indicates where the datagram should arrive. The data field is where all of actual content we want transmitted is placed. That content could be a video, an image, a text file, pretty much anything that can be represented in 1s and 0s. IP itself doesn't care what the content is. This freedom, of course, comes at a price: Just as we can send and receive cat videos, malevolent actors can send malicious content — viruses, worms, Trojan horses, phishing attacks, scams, and false information.

The Web & Web APIs

JavaScript & Webpages

From our our understanding of networks, we know the following: When a user types a URL into their browser's address bar and hits enter, an HTTP request is sent to the server hosting that webpage. That server then sends a response back to the browser. Let's say the the URL entered is to an HTML file on that server, and let's say that HTML file displays a small registration form for some event. When the user completes the fields and hits “submit”, another request is sent to the server. What the server does with that request depends on what the programmer has coded for when that particular request is received. The server might verify the field's contents, store the data, or return a new page. It's entirely up the programmer. Nevertheless, once it's done, the server will send a response, like a confirmation page (or at least it should; it would be a poor website for the submit button not to display any output when it's clicked).

Where does JavaScript fit in this picture? JavaScript provides the dynamic, or reactive, nature of a webpage. It can also, however, be used to handle the server side code. It's most common use case, however, is to add more dynamic elements to a webpage. Webpages based purely off of HTML and CSS are generally called static webpages. We can think of static webpages as akin to PDF files (but they can be more sophisticated, with CSS animations, buttons, and links). The webpages don't exactly change their structures in response to user interaction. JavaScript is what transforms the static webpage into a dynamic webpage: A webpage that changes its structure and content depending on the user or the user's actions.

For example, consider a webpage that only displays some blog content. When you're in Sendai, the content is in Japanese. When you're in Warsaw, it's in Polish. And when you're in Nuku'alofa, it's in Tongan. This is an example of a dynamic webpage — one whose content changes depending on some external factor. In this case, the user's location. If instead the webpage dispalyed English wherever you went — nothing else — it would be accurately described as a static webpage.

Now, it's not necessarily the case that JavaScript is what provides the language translation services for the webpage (it could be another language like PHP), but JavaScript is a possibility. This phenomenon of dynamically changing webpages is what JavaScript is used most often for.

JavaScript Libraries & Frameworks

Because of how pervasive JavaScript is in web development, JavaScript has one of the largest collections of libraries and frameworks, or more broadly, codebases. This is both a gift and a curse. For starters, when we work on large programs, we should always consider if a particular problem is already solved by an codebase. More often than not, the thorniest problems have existing solutions.

A helpful resource for quickly finding and researching existing codebases is the website JavaScripting.com.

Although this can significantly cut down development time, it can also lead to unexpected, and potentially disastrous, bugs. These bugs can be extraordinarily difficult to exterminate. A framework for dynamically reordering some buttons might rely on the assumption that containers can overflow, but if we globally restrict containers not to overflow, the framework may fail or cause problems elsewhere. Worse, that assumption might not be explicitly stated in the codebase. The resulting bug would be painfully difficult to find unless we understand the codebase. Accordingly, before using any codebase, we must always take the time to study the codebase thoroughly.

The second problem with so many codebases is relying on a codebase when plain JavaScript — often called vanilla JavaScript — would suffice. The most obvious example of this problem is using jQuery — a JavaScript framework intended to reduce some of the more verbose aspects of vanilla JavaScript. One of the more annoying aspects of JavaScript is trying to find solutions to a particular problem in vanilla JavaScript, only to find a plethora of results employing jQuery. Fortunately, with modern JavaScript's improvements, jQuery is slowly going away in favor of vanilla JavaScript. This all to say: Err on the side of using vanilla JavaScript whenever possible, but don't reinvent the wheel.
