Software Design

Unlike the others, this volume focuses more so on best practices in programming. Topics including approaches to naming, writing functions, classes, structures, and other data structures, as well as software design patterns in a variety of environments. As such, much of the topics covered here are language agnostic; they apply broadly to most programming languages.

Computer science isn't just an academic discipline. It's also an applied field, and many of the topics we learn are intended to solve real-world problems. Coming up with a solution, however, is only half the battle. There is still the aspect of implementation, and as with any other problem-solving discipline, there are guidelines we can follow to make both ours, and others', lives easier.

Software design practices are often domain-specific. Whether that domain is a language, a problem, or a data model, some practices are more readily applied in one situation over the next. Accordingly, the ability to spot and remedy implementation issues is a skill in itself. That ability is bolstered by examining what some domains identify to be issues and their approaches to resolution. These materials intend to provide that examination — we present common bad practices in a given domain and provide various approaches to correcting such habits.

We emphasize that the the materials here are purely guidelines and tips. In essence, advice. And as with any advice, it is always at our discretion whether to heed or reject. As we work through a problem, we might learn that the guideline we're following is a poor choice, more cumbersome than for what it's worth, or simply without benefit. Nevertheless, it can help to have some starting point, from which better ideas ensue.
